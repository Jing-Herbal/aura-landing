Calm tea: three herbs the Chinese kitchen has steeped for centuries

By the Jing team· 16 June 2026· 3 min read· 5 min prep + 15 min steep

甘草 × 龍眼 × 紅棗

Three dried ingredients, hot water, and a quarter of an hour. This is the tea we reach for at the end of a long day: naturally sweet without a grain of sugar, and built from three herbs the Chinese kitchen has been steeping together for centuries.

What you need

Makes one pot (about two cups):

  • 2–3 slices dried liquorice root 甘草
  • A small handful of dried longan 龍眼
  • 3–4 dried jujubes (red dates) 紅棗, torn or halved
  • Optional: 2–3 coins of fresh ginger 薑
  • About 600 ml of just-boiled water

All three dried ingredients are easy to find at any Asian grocer, and increasingly in the health-food aisle.

Why these three

Liquorice root is the great harmoniser of the Chinese pharmacopoeia; it appears in more classical formulas than almost any other herb, traditionally used to round off and balance a blend. Practically, it's also what makes this tea taste gently sweet without any added sugar.

Longan, the "dragon eye" fruit, is traditionally taken to nourish the blood and settle the spirit, which is why it turns up in so many evening soups and teas. It steeps into a mellow, honeyed base note.

Jujube is the everyday red date of the Chinese kitchen, the classic partner to longan in calming brews. Tearing the dates open before steeping lets the fruit give its body and sweetness to the water.

Ginger, if you add it, brings a little warmth to the cup and keeps the sweetness from sitting too heavy: nice in winter, easy to skip in summer.

How to make it

Rinse the dried ingredients briefly. Tear the jujubes open. Add everything to a teapot, pour over just-boiled water, and steep for 15 minutes, or simmer gently for 10 if you like a deeper brew. The pot re-steeps happily once or twice more.

A note on honesty

These herbs are described here by their traditional use, not by clinical claims. That's the same standard we hold Aura's label to. One practical caution: liquorice root contains glycyrrhizin, so keep this as an occasional cup rather than a daily habit, and skip it if you're pregnant or managing high blood pressure.
